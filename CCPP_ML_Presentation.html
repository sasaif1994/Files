The Target Variable

PE (Net Hourly Electrical Energy Output) — measured in megawatts (MW), this represents the total electricity generated by the Combined Cycle Power Plant per hour. A CCPP uses both gas turbines and steam turbines. Ambient conditions like temperature and pressure significantly affect turbine efficiency, making this a classic ML regression problem.

ML Task Identification
1
Task Type: Regression
PE (output) is a continuous number in MW — not a category. This makes it a supervised regression problem.
2
Evaluation Metric: RMSE
Root Mean Squared Error is in the same units as the target (MW), making it easy to interpret. Lower is better.
3
Secondary Metric: R²
Proportion of variance explained. An R² of 0.96 means the model explains 96% of the variation in power output.
4
Also Computed: MAE
Mean Absolute Error gives the average error in MW. Less sensitive to outliers than RMSE.

What does RMSE = 3.25 MW mean?

3.25 MW

The model's predictions are off by an average of 3.25 MW. Since the output range is ~75 MW (420–496 MW), this is only a 4.3% relative error — excellent performance for a real-world engineering dataset.

What does R² = 0.9637 mean?

96.4%

The model explains 96.4% of all variance in power output. Only 3.6% of variation is unexplained — possibly due to unmeasured factors like equipment wear or fuel quality.

Why Random Forest beat Linear Regression

Linear Regression assumes a straight-line relationship between features and PE. The actual relationship is non-linear — e.g., efficiency doesn't decrease linearly with temperature. Random Forest captures these curves automatically, reducing RMSE from 4.50 to 3.25 MW (a 28% improvement).

Key Insight: Temperature Dominates

Temperature (AT) accounts for 90.1% of predictive importance in the Random Forest. This aligns with thermodynamics: the Carnot efficiency of gas turbines drops sharply with higher ambient temperature. The other 3 features contribute only 9.9% combined.
